Ken Shenstone

Wood Fired Ceramic Studio & Albion Anagama
"Though not random, surfaces and even changes to the form are unpredictable. The best results are often unplanned."

In the countryside outside Albion, Michigan, down a quiet road, sits something extraordinary: the largest single-chamber wood-fired kiln in North America. It was built in 1987 by Ken Shenstone and David Habicht from reclaimed industrial refractory brick salvaged from the Albion Malleable Iron Company — a Japanese anagama tradition constructed with Michigan industrial heritage.

For nearly 40 years, Ken has been firing this kiln, loading 2,000+ pieces at a time, feeding the fire for 10 continuous days with a team of a dozen people, then waiting two weeks for it to cool. The results are unpredictable by design — wood ash settles on clay surfaces and, through the complex interaction of flame, ash, and earth, forms natural glazes that no human hand could replicate.

Philosophy

Wood ash settles on the pottery during the firing, and the complex interaction between flame, ash and clay forms a natural ash glaze. Just like life, it's unpredictable. One piece can come out dark yellow, with a variety of textures, while another will end up with a smooth light teal finish, depending on its placement. — Ken Shenstone
