What Is an Anagama?

An anagama (穴窯) is a Japanese "cave kiln" — one of the oldest kiln designs in the world. It is a single-chamber structure, typically built on a hillside, where the fire enters at the lowest point and travels upward through a long chamber filled with pottery, exiting through a chimney at the far end. As the fire burns over days, wood ash is carried by the flame and settles on the surfaces of the pots, creating natural ash glazes — no applied glaze needed. The placement of each piece within the kiln determines what surfaces, colors, and textures it will develop.

Ken's kiln in Albion takes this ancient form and builds it at extraordinary scale — 55 feet from end to end, making it the largest single-chamber wood-fired kiln in North America.

Construction

The Albion Anagama was built in 1987 by Ken Shenstone and David Habicht. The construction materials tell a story of place:

  • Reclaimed industrial refractory brick from the Malleable Forge — the former Albion Malleable Iron Company, a piece of Albion's industrial past repurposed for art
  • Fieldstone buttresses — local stone providing structural support

The design blends traditional Japanese hill-climbing kiln architecture with local Michigan industrial heritage. It's not a replica of a Japanese kiln — it's something that could only exist in Albion, built from what was there.

Ken has built four different kilns total on the property. Artists from around the world come to fire their work in them. Adjacent to the kilns sits a 4,000 square foot ceramics gallery, also built by Ken and David Habicht.

How It Works

The fire starts with small kindling at the front of the kiln — the lowest point. Over the course of 10 continuous days (a 240-hour cycle), the kiln transforms from a cold, dark, cave-like space to a red and glowing inferno. A river of fire travels from the front up through the length of the kiln and out the chimney, depositing ash on every pottery surface it passes.

Fuel

The kiln is fired with wood donated by Albion resident Rusty Hull, who receives artwork in return — a barter system that embodies the community nature of this project. At peak heat, the kiln consumes up to one face cord (4×8 feet) of wood per half hour.

The Team

Roughly 12 people work round-the-clock shifts to keep the fire fed for the full 10-day cycle. This is not a solo endeavor — it is, by necessity and by choice, a collaborative event. People bring meals, split wood, tend the fire, and talk. The conversations that start on day one don't stop until the last ember is banked.

After the Fire

When the firing ends, the kiln must cool for two full weeks before it can be opened. What comes out are surfaces shaped by 240 hours of flame, ash deposit, and atmospheric chemistry — natural ash glazes ranging from dark yellow with complex textures to smooth light teal, all determined by where each piece sat inside the kiln.

After cooling and unloading, Ken hosts studio sales at the property, typically in autumn. Prices range from as low as $8 to hundreds — making the work accessible while honoring the extraordinary process behind each piece.

The Other Kilns

The Albion Anagama is the flagship — but it's one of four kilns on the property. Ken also built a noborigama ("Bobo the Nobo"), a dome-tube hybrid of his own design, and at least one additional kiln. Each represents a different philosophy of fire.
