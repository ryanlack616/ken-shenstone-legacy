Studio Life

Community, fire, and conversations that last for days

A Collaborative Fire

Sort of a subdued party atmosphere that lasts for 10 days. Conversations go on that people realize are going to go on for 24 hours a day. It's not uncommon to have an interesting conversation, go to bed, wake up eight hours later and that conversation is still going on. — Ken Shenstone

The Albion Anagama is not — and has never been — a solo project. A 10-day continuous firing requires roughly 12 people working in shifts, feeding the fire around the clock, bringing meals, splitting wood, supervising the temperature, and keeping each other company through long nights lit by the glow from the kiln mouth.

This collaborative nature is not accidental. The kiln is designed to require community. Other artists bring meals, split wood, or help supervise the fire in return for space in the kiln for their own work. What stays consistent across nearly 40 years is the need to work together and the energy that surrounds each firing.

Artists from around the world have fired their work in Ken's kilns. The studio at 29005 Albion Road is a destination for potters who want to understand wood firing at a scale few other places in the world can offer.

Programs

Resident Artist

Ken's studio hosts a resident artist program, offering potters the chance to work alongside a master and fire in kilns that most people only read about. Living and working on the property means immersion in the daily rhythms of a working ceramic studio — from morning throwing sessions to preparing for the next firing.

Apprenticeships

For those looking to learn deeply, Ken offers apprenticeship positions. As recently as September 2025, he was posting to ceramics communities:

Woodfire!!! Sometimes opportunities come along that will change your life forever. This is your opportunity to really learn to understand... — Ken Shenstone, September 2025, Pots by Potters

The Wild Clay Pottery community described the opportunity as a chance to "work in one of the top ranked wood-fire centers in the world."

Community Firings

The firings are open to visiting artists. Bring meals, split wood, help supervise the fire — and earn space in the kiln for your own work. This is how the kiln has always operated: as a shared resource, powered by collective effort.

The Property

The studio at 29005 Albion Road is more than a workspace — it's a compound Ken built for the work. He constructed the studio building himself, including crafting and installing the windows. The property includes:

  • The Albion Anagama — 55 feet, the largest in North America
  • Three additional kilns Ken has built over the decades
  • A 4,000 square foot ceramics gallery (built with David Habicht)
  • The hand-built studio with handmade windows
  • Resident artist and apprentice living space

Documentary

Events & History

1987
First firing of the Albion Anagama. Ken Shenstone and David Habicht complete the kiln using reclaimed refractory brick from the Malleable Forge.
October 2010
MLive / Citizen Patriot publishes feature: "Anagama kiln in Albion is largest of its kind in the United States."
July 2013
Ken and Anne Beyer demonstrate wheel throwing at the Chelsea Sounds & Sights Festival Art Market.
2015–2016
Dave Lawrence films the Albion Anagama documentary.
June 2017
30th anniversary of the kiln celebrated on Facebook. Community calls Ken "one of the best anagama potters around."
October 2019
Studio included in the Arts and Eats self-guided tour across Southwest Michigan.
March 2022
CultureVerse creates Matterport 3D scans of the kiln and pottery.
September 2025
Ken posts apprenticeship opportunity — studio still active after nearly 40 years.
