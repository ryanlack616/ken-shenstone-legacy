The refractory connection

This page isn't about ceramics. It's about a material — and the two men, 50 miles apart in the same state, who used it to maintain fire.

NARCO Ram

What It Is

NARCO Ram is a castable refractory ramming mix made by the North American Refractories Company. It's a high-temperature material used to line and repair furnaces, boilers, kilns — anything that holds extreme heat. You mix it, pack it into the gaps and joints and worn places, and it cures into a rock-hard surface that can withstand thousands of degrees.

It's not glamorous. It's not the kind of material anyone writes about. It's maintenance material — the stuff that keeps the fire contained, the structure sound, the heat where it needs to be. The people who use it are the people who keep things running.

The Albion Kilns

  • Where: 29005 Albion Road, Albion, MI
  • Who: Ken Shenstone
  • Duration: Nearly 4 decades
  • Schedule: When the kiln needs fire
  • Location: Rural Albion, ~50 miles west of home
  • Work: Kiln construction and refractory maintenance
  • Material: NARCO Ram — used at transition zones, joint repairs, stoke hole surrounds, firebox lining

Two Men, One Material

Ryan's father drove 50 miles to Zug Island to keep a boiler running. Ken drives nowhere — the kilns are on his property — but the work is the same work: maintaining the integrity of a structure that holds fire. When refractory degrades from thermal cycling — the endless expansion and contraction as a furnace heats and cools — someone has to pack the gaps, seal the joints, keep the heat where it belongs. That someone uses NARCO Ram.

At Number Two Boiler, the fire was industrial. It made steam that made power that made steel. The fire was not the point; it was the engine.

At Albion, the fire is the point. It makes ash that makes glaze that makes art. The fire is not an engine — it is the artist's collaborator, the unpredictable partner that Ken has spent 40 years learning to work with.

But the material that maintains both fires is the same. The hands that pack it into the joints are doing the same work. And neither man — the boiler operator on swing shift or the ceramic artist at his kiln — would call what he does anything other than necessary.

What This Is Not

The brick in Ken's kilns is not from Zug Island. It comes from the Albion Malleable Iron Company — the former "Malleable Forge," right there in Albion. That's a different story: local industrial heritage repurposed for art, Albion's past becoming Albion's present.

The connection between Ken's kilns and Ryan's father is not the brick. It is the NARCO Ram. It is the practice of refractory maintenance — the unglamorous, essential work of keeping the fire contained. One man did it for industry. One man does it for art. They never met. They used the same material.

Though not random, surfaces and even changes to the form are unpredictable. The best results are often unplanned. — Ken Shenstone

The same could be said of the connections we find in life.

This page was written by Ryan, who visited Ken's studio on the insistence of his friend Henry Crissman, and recognized the NARCO Ram on the kiln walls. The connection was immediate and personal. Not everything in a ceramic legacy is about ceramics.
