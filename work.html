Work

Each piece shaped by hand, finished by fire

Ken's pottery is exclusively wood-fired in the Albion Anagama. No commercial glazes are applied — every surface, color, and texture comes from the interaction of flame, wood ash, and clay over 10 continuous days of firing. The placement of each piece within the 55-foot kiln determines its final character.

Though not random, surfaces and even changes to the form are unpredictable. The best results are often unplanned.— Ken Shenstone

Prices range from as low as $8 to hundreds — Ken has always made his work accessible. Pieces are available at studio sales following each firing (typically autumn) and have been displayed year-round at the Chelsea Ale House and at the ClayHandi Restaurant in Kenmore.

Forms

Ken worked in a range of traditional and inventive forms, each uniquely finished by 240 hours of fire and ash:

Teabowls

The chawan — the form most intimately connected to the Japanese wood-firing tradition. Each one unique, its surface determined entirely by its journey through fire and ash.

Moon Jars

Large, round vessels inspired by the Korean dal hangari — simple in form but transformed by the kiln's atmosphere into surfaces of remarkable complexity.

Bud Vases

Small, intimate forms designed to hold a single stem or small arrangement. The scale makes each ash-glazed surface a landscape in miniature.

Flower Vases

Larger vase forms, each one carrying distinct marks of flame path and ash deposit based on its position in the kiln.

Lidded Jars

Functional covered vessels — the lid and body fired together, their surfaces matched by shared placement yet individually marked by ash flow.

Flip Top Bottles

Bottles with hinged or flip-top closures — playful functional forms where Ken's woodworking skills complement his ceramic work.

Scrolls

Sculptural ceramic scrolls — forms referencing the Japanese aesthetic tradition while being entirely products of fire and earth.

Peanut Vessels

Organic, peanut-shaped vessels — rounded double forms that play with volume and the way ash glazes respond to concave and convex surfaces.
